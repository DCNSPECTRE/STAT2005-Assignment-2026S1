Perth’s freeway network has undergone major changes in recent years. One of the more controversial upgrades has been the Smart Freeways project; a modification which aims to improve the travel time of commuters from Perth’s outer suburbs heading towards the city. This system has been implemented on both the Kwinana Freeway Northbound and the Mitchell Freeway Southbound, controlling the flow of traffic predominantly through digital speed-limit gantries and on-ramp metering. On the Mitchell South, this system saves 7-minutes when travelling from Hester Avenue to Vincent Street in the morning peak (Main Roads, REFERENCE). However, residents closer to the city are generally more impacted by ramp-metering, and, have noticed their morning commute more than doubling in length (ABC, REFERENCE).

In order to construct a queuing model, we can use this dataset to simulate the incoming volume of traffic during the morning peak. Typically, this model would be formed on the Poisson distribution because of its memoryless property and ease of implementation. However, a Poisson distribution assumes an equal mean and variance, so it’s important that verify this assumption. To visualise, let’s filter the above for weekdays, the peak hours, and without public holidays (eg 06/03/23 – Labour Day), yielding the following:

We can see that the volume of traffic entering the freeway varies heavily over the course of the morning. It would be unreasonable to fit a single model to this level of variation, so instead consider a piecewise approach. Models that account for the minimum, maximum and mean volume each morning will be constructed, correlating to the times 6am, 7am and approximately 8am. We’ll focus on the 6:00-6:15am interval for now:

smu6  <- mean(ramp23.pk.wk[ramp23.pk.wk$Period == "06:00", 4])
svar6 <- var(ramp23.pk.wk[ramp23.pk.wk$Period == "06:00", 4])

data.frame (

  Variance = svar6,
  row.names = c("6am (min)")

              Mean Variance
6am (min) 113.2273  161.803

Even still, this is an overdispersed dataset where the variance of volume is considerably higher than its mean. This is true throughout the entire dataset, meaning a Poisson distribution would be unsuitable as we do not satisfy the assumption of equidispersion. In order to provide a better fit, we can consider a Negative Binomial where both a mu and a size parameter (for dispersion) can be selected. (NEED TO TALK ABOUT HOW MEMORYLESS IS STILL INVALID) We can calculate this size parameter \(r\) as follows (REFERENCEEEE):
